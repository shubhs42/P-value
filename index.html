An interactive, beginner-friendly explainer. Build intuition step by step.

Starting point

What does a distribution look like?

💡 Imagine measuring the heights of 1,000 students. Most are close to the average. Very few are extremely short or extremely tall. If you plotted every height, you'd get a bell-shaped curve — that's a distribution.

The peak is where most values land. The tails are where rare values live. Move further from the center and values become increasingly uncommon.

⚠️ The x-axis shows possible values (like height). The y-axis shows how common each value is. The curve is just a picture of how data spreads out.
PDF — Probability Density Function

The PDF — "how dense is this spot?"

💡 Think of the PDF like a crowd density map. Where the curve is tall, lots of values are packed in. Where it's flat, values are sparse. It tells you: "How crowded is this region?"

Drag the slider to pick any point x. The orange dot shows the PDF value — the height of the curve at that point. Notice it's tallest in the center and nearly zero at the extremes.

x = 0.00
f(x) = height of the curve at point x

⚠️ f(x) is NOT a probability — it's a density value.
✅ Probability = the AREA between two points under the curve.
Key insight: If you add up all the area under the PDF (from −∞ to +∞), you always get exactly 1. Because something must always happen!
CDF — Cumulative Distribution Function

The CDF — "what fraction is below x?"

💡 Imagine lining up all 1,000 students by height. The CDF asks: "If I stand at height x, what fraction of students are shorter than me?"

At the average → 50% are shorter. Very tall → ~99% are shorter. Very short → nearly 0%.

Move the slider. The blue shaded area on the left chart grows — that's the area being accumulated. The right chart plots this accumulated value directly as an S-shaped curve.

x = 0.00
PDF — area accumulating
CDF — F(x) = accumulated area
F(x) = P(X ≤ x) = area under PDF from −∞ to x

F(−∞) = 0.00 → nothing is below −∞
F( 0 ) = 0.50 → 50% are below the mean
F(+∞) = 1.00 → everything is below +∞

And: f(x) = d/dx F(x) [PDF = derivative of CDF]
P-value

The P-value — "how surprising is my result?"

💡 You run an experiment and get a result. You ask: "If nothing special was going on (null hypothesis H₀), how likely is it I'd get a result THIS extreme just by chance?"

That probability is the p-value. Think of it as a surprise meter.

🟢 Large p-value → not surprising → no evidence against H₀
🔴 Small p-value → very surprising → strong evidence against H₀

Drag the slider to move your observed result. Watch the red tail area — that's the p-value. The further into the tail, the more surprising, the smaller the p-value.

z = 2.00
p = 0.0228 — Rare! Strong evidence against H₀
The standard threshold is α = 0.05. If p < 0.05, we say the result is "statistically significant." This is a convention — not a law of nature!
Three types of p-value

Upper tail, lower tail, and two tails

💡 The type of p-value you use depends on your research question:

▶ "Is the effect bigger than expected?" → right (upper) tail
◀ "Is the effect smaller than expected?" → left (lower) tail
◀▶ "Is the effect different in any direction?" → both tails
z = 1.65
P-value = P(Z ≥ z | H₀) = 1 − F(z) [upper tail]
p = 0.0495
Full picture

Everything in one view

💡 PDF → CDF → p-value is one continuous story. The PDF is the curve's shape. The CDF accumulates area from the left. The p-value is a specific tail area at your observed data point.
📐
PDF f(x)
Height of the curve. Shows where values are dense. Not a probability itself.
📈
CDF F(x)
Area to the left of x. Gives P(X ≤ x). Starts at 0, ends at 1.
🚨
P-value
Area in the tail beyond your observed z. How surprising is the result?
z = 1.65
PDF height f(z)
0.1023
CDF value F(z)
0.9505
P-value (right tail)
0.0495
The relationship in one line:

PDF ──[integrate from −∞ to x]──▶ CDF
CDF ──[evaluate at z, take tail ]──▶ p-value

p = 1 − F(z) [right tail, most common]
p = F(z) [left tail]
p = 2·(1−F(|z|)) [two-tailed]
